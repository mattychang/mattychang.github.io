Research Summary

I conducted my first research project alongside Sarah Kohrt, formerly a PhD candidate and now a postdoc at UNC Chapel Hill, whose thesis focused on characterizing an androgen receptor (AR) antagonist (BG-15a). My specific project focused on investigating the localization of the AR after treatment with BG-15a. Using biotinylated-isoxazole (b-isox), a compound that binds to the intrinsically disordered regions of the AR and precipitates out at high concentrations, I showed through Western blots that the AR is insoluble at higher concentrations of b-isox after treatment with BG-15a. Coupled with previous findings, I validated that the AR localizes into the nucleus but represses cell proliferation and gene expression after BG-15a drug treatment, suggesting a new mechanism of action in which the AR binds to chromatin but does not activate transcription. This project led to one publication in PNAS.

More recently, I've been working with Imdadul Khan, a postdoc, to characterize a novel and potent CBP/p300 inhibitor (IHK-44) in alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (aRMS), a rare pediatric cancer driven by a fusion protein PAX3-FOXO1 (P3F). After the recent discovery that P3F recruits the histone acetyltrafersases CBP/p300 to its C-terminal activation domain by a cysteine reside, C793, we showed that IHK-44 reduces histone acetylation, selectively downregulates P3F target genes, and slows down aRMS proliferation. Furthermore, we discovered that IHK-44, along with other CBP/p300 epigenetic small molecules are more sensitive in aRMS than in embryonal RMS (eRMS) and non-cancerous myoblast cell lines. This led us to believe that there may be greater dependency on CBP/p300 and deposits of histone acetylation in aRMS than in eRMS. By looking at baseline histone acetylation levels, we showed that H2B N-terminus acetylation (H2BNTac) may serve as a biomarker for enhancer-addicted cancers, like MYCN-amplified neuroblastoma and AR+ prostate cancer. This vulnerability may serve as a predictor of cancer cell vulnerability to CBP/p300 inhibition in the clinic. We've submitted this work to Molecular Cell where it is currently in review.

Currently, I am piloting a new project aimed at directly targeting the essential cysteine residue in the C-terminal domain of P3F. A purely biochemical approach was not pursued, as purification of P3F is hindered by its intrinsically disordered nature. To circumvent these challenges, I implemented a luciferase reporter assay paired with a counter-screen to evaluate a cysteine-focused covalent library. This effort yielded 376 compounds capable of disrupting P3F-driven transcription. Building on these results, I established a hit-to-lead pipeline incorporating orthogonal assays: cell viability assays to assess cytotoxicity and potency, cellular thermal shift assays to confirm direct target engagement, competitive activity-based protein profiling to evaluate proteome-wide selectivity, and fluorescence-based monitoring of endogenously tagged P3F to track protein stability and degradation, and purification of that cell line by IP-MS to map peptide-wide selectivity. More work is necessary to establish the binding and covalency mechanisms of the lead, as well as assessing the phenotypic effects and translational potential of the lead.
